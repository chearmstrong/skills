# Review Checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing existing writing. Report only issues that matter for the requested scope.

## Accuracy

- Are technical claims supported by source material?
- Are product names, commands, API names, paths, and identifiers exact?
- Are version, date, benchmark, and dependency claims current enough for the context?
- Are limitations, failure modes, permissions, costs, and operational tradeoffs visible near the claims they qualify?
- Do examples demonstrate the stated behaviour, or are they decorative?

## Structure

- Does the title promise what the piece delivers?
- Is the audience clear from the opening section?
- Does the order match the reader's task or learning path?
- Are prerequisites before steps?
- Are conceptual sections separated from procedures and reference material?
- Could headings be scanned as a useful outline?

## Clarity

- Are important subjects named rather than hidden behind "it", "this", or passive phrasing?
- Are acronyms expanded on first use where appropriate?
- Are code blocks, commands, and outputs labelled clearly?
- Are links descriptive enough without surrounding context?
- Does each example state what the reader should learn from it?

## Tone

- Is the voice direct, concrete, and technically honest?
- Does the draft avoid hype, vague confidence, or exaggerated certainty?
- Does it sound like shared engineering experience rather than marketing unless marketing copy was requested?
- Does it respect the reader's time by removing throat-clearing and repeated caveats?

## Documentation Types

For tutorials, check that the reader reaches a working result.

For how-to guides, check that steps are actionable, ordered, and verifiable.

For reference material, check that labels, options, defaults, and constraints are complete and consistent.

For explanations, check that tradeoffs and causality are clear.

For release notes, check that user impact, affected versions, and action required are explicit.

For blog posts, check that the hook is honest, the narrative has a through-line, and technical claims remain grounded.

## Output Discipline

- Lead with the highest-risk findings.
- Separate correctness, publication-safety, structure, and copy-editing concerns.
- Quote only the smallest necessary excerpt.
- Suggest replacement wording where it reduces back-and-forth.
- Say when no substantive issues were found, and name any residual review gap.
